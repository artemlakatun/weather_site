From a fundamental point of view, weather is a dynamic system in which small inaccuracies in the initial conditions can lead to large inaccuracies in the output. This is called the “butterfly effect”: the flapping of a butterfly’s wings in Tokyo will lead to a hurricane in New York, and if you don’t take this flapping into account, you may miss the hurricane. The taxonomy of global climate change is very complex: there is no single trend, be it global warming or global cooling. When this summer turns out to be hotter than the previous one, or even when several years in a row are unusually hot, this is rather a random process. There are projects where people summarize point observations from weather stations and, based on this data, find out which summer was the hottest or which winter was the coldest on planet Earth. And some local peaks, such as the hottest day in the entire history of meteorological observations in Moscow, are just coincidences. If you determine the hottest day, then you need to analyze meteorological observations not for 50, but for 500 years: only such a scale allows us to talk about climate change.
